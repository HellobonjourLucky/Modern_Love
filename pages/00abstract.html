According to Maslow, love and belongingness are one of the innate needs of human beings.1 Thus, human connection has played a significant role throughout history in various contexts. And in traditional context, building connections involved physicality, such as face-to-face conversations or hugs.

However, we are currently witnessing a Renaissance of human connection through technology. Platforms like Tinder, representing the online dating market, have introduced a new dating culture, with algorithms serving as matchmakers to help us find true love. And these matchmakers recommend potential matches based on individuals' preferences and behavioral data, expanding our social bubble.

But it is true that Tinder, which represents the online dating markets, opens a new chapter of dating industry with the algorithmic datafication and it has a huge impact on users‚Äô relationship. Such as, distance and location play an important role in this dating application, as the application prioritizes matches based on proximity, increasing chances in-person meetings based on users‚Äô location data. But we easily forget the ramifications. For example, the cyber space allows simultaneous conversations with multiple people at the same time, challenging commitment in one conversation or a deeper relationship. As a result, these interactions often have restrictions and can even pose risks. Because these easy interactions may transform romantic relationships into a seemingly mindless activity.

Yet, various factors, including social networking services, behavioral addiction, algorithms, and online conversations without physical meetups, can influence our decisions and relationships. Therefore, to gain a deeper understanding of modern relationships, it is crucial to be more conscious about the journey of how we fall in love via a machine.
